Genome evolution of Solanaceae

     Solanaceae has over 90 genera and nearly 3000 species, which distributed throughout the globe, with a great diversity of ecology and morphology. It is an economically important family, among which are tomato, potato, eggplant, tobacco, chili and petunia. We believe genome innovations created genetic plasticity which brought great success of Solanaceae in the current world.

     Our research is to understand what innovations occurred in Solanaceae genomes over the past 60 million years, and how they have helped Solanaceae adapt to vast climate changes and make them interested to the humans. Facilitated by the availability of whole-genome assembly and transcriptomic data, we have generated an integrated database of Solanaceae omics. Based on that, we couple bioinformatics and molecular biology approaches to investigate the role and process of genome evolution in Solanaceae.

Sequencing and annotation of mango and cashew genomes and transcriptomes

     Mango is one of the most widely cultivated fruits in tropics, and the tropical cashew tree produces nuts which are widely consumed in diverse ways. Both belong to the genus Mangifera. Despite their economic importance in tropics, molecular biology research and their biotechnological applications have lagged far behind other major crops, which seriously hampered sustainable development of mango and cashew industries.

     We have sequenced and assembled mango genome, and re-sequenced over 30 mango cultivars. Also, we have sequenced transcriptomes for more than 20 tissues samples. Meanwhile, we have sequenced and assembled transcriptomes for over 10 cashew tissues/developmental stages. Annotation of the data is under way.

Evolution of unique traits and pathways in eukaryotes

Unique traits and metabolic pathways are coded by genomic features that particular organisms invented. We are working to decode evolutionary mechanisms that shaped the unique traits and pathways in eukaryotes. Particularly, we are interested in lipid and glycan metabolism pathways in eukaryotes. Thousands of genes have been identified and grouped based on their conserved domains, and several new subfamilies were discovered. Diverse mechanisms of gene innovation, like sequence divergence, domain invention and domain shuffling, were found to be involved in shaping the toolkit for the unique pathways.
